Baby (9-11 Months.)

By 11 months, your baby may become more independent and she may be able to eat or drink on her own. Keep watching the baby close while she is eating in order to avoid choking. Don’t worry if the baby is not ready to eat on her own! Just help and encourage by offering finger foods and practice her to hold the spoon.

At a glance

• Your baby should be joining in the family meal and tucking into three small meals a day
• They can enjoy what everyone else is having now – just chopped into baby-sized bites
• As they are on the move now, offer them healthy snacks to keep their energy up.

Here’s a guide to the nutritious meals, snacks and crucial nutrients your active baby needs to stay healthy and full of energy;


1. Starchy foods:


i.) Potatoes.

ii.) Bread/toast or Breakfast Cereal.



iii.) Pasta rice.


Starchy foods Like potatoes, pasta rice, bread/toast or breakfast cereal – include them at all meals for that all important energy boost


3. Full-fat dairy foods:


i.) Cheese.

ii.) Yoghurt.



iii.) Fromage Frais(White Cheese).

iv.) Milk Puddings.


Foods like cheese, yoghurt, fromage frais(white cheese) and milk puddings are packed with calcium, which is ideal for strong bones and teeth.
Studies also show that the fat can slow down the body's absorption of sugar, helping lower the risk of diabetes. Research published by Kenyan Academy of Pediatrics (KAP) analyzed on the health benefits of eating low-fat and full-fat dairy products, involving almost 1 million babies from around the world. It found that consuming milk, yogurt, cheese and other dairy products — either low-fat or full-fat — had no impact on rates of cardiovascular disease, coronary heart disease, or deaths from any cause.
The total fat content of dairy products can vary a lot. To make healthier choices, look at the nutrition information on the label to check the amount of fat, including saturated fat, salt and sugar, in the dairy products you're choosing.
The fat in milk provides calories for young babies, and also contains essential vitamins. Although, it's a good idea to go for lower fat milks because having too much fat can result in your baby becoming overweight.

2. Fruit and vegetables:


i.) Tinned Fruits

ii.) Frozen Fruits


iii.) Dried Fruits

iv.) Fresh Fruits

Babies bewteen the months of nine and eleven should be fed Vegies and fruits including tinned, frozen and dried as well as fresh fruits include them in all your baby’s meals.


4. Proteinases foods:

i.) Meat

ii.) Tofu(Bean Curd)

iii.) Fish

iv.) Eggs

v.) Pulses(Lentils, Beans or Peas)

vi.) Nut Butters

Babies bewteen the months of nine and eleven should be fed Meat, tofu(bean curd), fish, eggs, pulses (lentils, beans or peas) or nut butters – include these at two meals each day to give you baby plenty of iron

5. Finger Foods:

This is food served in such a form and style that it can conveniently be eaten with the fingers. You can now offer hard finger foods such as: Breadsticks, Rice cakes, Crackers (biscuits), Toast with cheese spread Pieces of raw fruit and vegetables such as apples, carrots, cucumber, Cubes of cheese and Pieces of boiled egg.

6. Iron-Rich Foods:

Your baby was born with stores of iron, but they start to run out from around six months. Lack of iron is the most common nutritional deficiency seen in young children, so it’s very important to offer your baby plenty of those iron-rich foods. Good sources of iron include:
a) Red meat – like beef and lamb
b) Chicken and turkey - especially the dark meat
c) Dried fruit – like prunes and apricots
d) Iron-fortified cereals – as long as they’re low sugar
e) Eggs - cooked till both the white and yolk are solid
f) Beans, tofu (bean curd), lentils and chickpeas
g) Nut butters
h) Green vegetables – like spinach, broccoli and peas
i) Red meat – like beef and lamb
j) Keep your baby’s diet healthy – find out how to avoid giving the wrong foods.



HOW MUCH QUANTITY PER DAY?


For the quantity of food, always be guided with your baby’s hunger as appetite vary from baby to baby, and most babies change from day to day. Here is an approximate amount:
• Cereal – 1/4 to 1/2 cup
• Vegetables – 1/4 to 1/2 cup
• Fruits – 1/4 to 1/2 cup
• Mixed Cereals – 1/4 to 1/2 cup
• Dairy – 2 to 3 tbsp.
• Meat/Protein – 4 tbsp

NB;
• Sterilize the bowls and spoons used for feeding your baby in a vessel with hot water for five minutes and keep it immersed in the same vessel until use.
• Never introduce more than 1 food at a time when introducing solids to your baby.
• Salt, sugar, cow’s milk and honey is not recommended in baby’s food until 1 years of age.
• Check for allergy symptoms when you introducing a new food to your baby and stop feeding if you find any symptoms.
• Feed your baby with breastmilk or formula milk on demand.
• Steaming is a healthy option for baby food than pressure cooking.
